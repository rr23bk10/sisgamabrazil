Why Open Innovation Cannot Be Led by Communities Controlled by SEBRAE, UTFPR, UENP and Other Public Institutions

By Renan Ricardo Pereira Lima • 23/11/2025

Open innovation has become one of the central pillars for new models of development, competitiveness and societal transformation. Governments, companies and educational institutions frequently adopt this concept as a banner — often without fully understanding what it requires in practice.

Across small and mid-sized cities, we see a growing pattern: most so-called “innovation communities” are controlled by public institutions such as SEBRAE, federal universities, state universities and government agencies. However, this structure directly contradicts the fundamental principles of real open innovation.

This article explains why these institutions may participate — but cannot lead or control genuine open innovation ecosystems.

1. What is open innovation, actually?

Open innovation is a model in which:

  • people, companies, startups, academia and society collaborate freely;
  • ideas circulate without institutional barriers;
  • no one owns or controls the ecosystem;
  • any actor can propose, test, fail and iterate quickly;
  • governance is decentralized and distributed.

It is based on four essential pillars:

  • Autonomy of the community
  • Diversity of actors and ideas
  • Absence of rigid hierarchy
  • Speed of experimentation
Any environment that violates these pillars does not practice open innovation — it merely simulates it.

2. Why SEBRAE, UTFPR, UENP and public institutions cannot lead open innovation

Although they are important actors in the ecosystem, their institutional nature makes it impossible for them to lead true open innovation.

2.1. Hierarchical and bureaucratic structure

SEBRAE, UTFPR and UENP operate within rigid structures defined by:

  • formal processes
  • internal regulations
  • committees and councils
  • legal reviews
  • accountability requirements
  • vertical organizational charts

Open innovation demands the opposite: fast decisions, flexibility and dynamism.

A community that depends on institutional approval to act can never experiment with the required speed.

2.2. Centralized governance: someone is always in control

In communities controlled by public institutions, there is always:

  • a central authority
  • an institutional agenda
  • filters for participation
  • limitations on what can or cannot be done

The presence of centralized control contradicts the very essence of open innovation.

2.3. The institutional agenda outweighs the community agenda

Public institutions operate according to political, budgetary and procedural goals — which rarely reflect the real needs of the ecosystem.

The institution becomes the protagonist, and the community becomes secondary.

2.4. Lack of real diversity of ideas

Institution-led communities tend to repeat the same mentors, the same professors, the same partners and the same recurring participants — which dramatically reduces diversity.

Diversity is not just demographic; it is diversity of method, speed, risk tolerance and strategic vision.

2.5. A culture of zero tolerance for risk

Public institutions avoid reputational risks, unguaranteed experiments and anything that may be seen as a failure. But open innovation is built exactly on risk and rapid experimentation.

2.6. Dependence on public funding

When sustainability depends on public budgets, grants, annual releases or bureaucratic approvals, the ecosystem cannot maintain momentum or autonomy.

3. The ideal role of these institutions

They should be:

  • partners
  • connectors
  • knowledge providers
  • supporting infrastructure
  • participants — not controllers

4. Open innovation only works from the bottom up

Real open innovation ecosystems are born from the community — not from institutions.

When institutions attempt to control the ecosystem, the result becomes:

  • institutional programs
  • political showcases
  • predictable events
  • hubs dependent on public funding

Conclusion

SEBRAE, UTFPR, UENP and similar public institutions may contribute — but they cannot lead. Open innovation requires:

  • decentralization
  • autonomy
  • diversity
  • speed
  • freedom
  • community leadership

Truly innovative ecosystems emerge from people, startups and local creators — not from bureaucratic structures.
